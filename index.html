What is Chemistry?

Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the study of matter, its properties, and the changes it undergoes.

Periodic Table

The periodic table arranges elements according to their atomic number, electronic configuration, and recurring chemical properties.

Acids and Bases

Acids release H⁺ ions in aqueous solution, while bases release OH⁻ ions. The strength of acids and bases is measured using the pH scale.

Salts

Definition of Salt

A salt is a chemical compound formed when an acid reacts with a base. This reaction is called a neutralization reaction.

Formation of Salts

Salts are produced by the combination of positive ions from bases and negative ions from acids. For example, when hydrochloric acid reacts with sodium hydroxide, sodium chloride (salt) is formed.

Example reaction:
HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

Types of Salts

Salts can be classified as neutral salts, acidic salts, or basic salts depending on the nature of the acid and base used in their formation.

Atom

Definition of Atom

An atom is the smallest unit of matter that retains the chemical properties of an element. Atoms combine with each other to form molecules and compounds.

Structure of an Atom

An atom consists of a central nucleus and electrons surrounding it. The nucleus contains positively charged protons and neutral neutrons. Negatively charged electrons revolve around the nucleus in energy levels called shells.

The number of protons in the nucleus is called the atomic number, and it determines the identity of an element.

Atomic Number

Definition

The atomic number is the number of protons present in the nucleus of an atom. In a neutral atom, this number is also equal to the number of electrons surrounding the nucleus.

Importance of Atomic Number

The atomic number identifies an element and determines its position in the periodic table. All atoms that have the same atomic number belong to the same element, even if their masses are different.

Relation with Mass Number

The mass number of an atom is the total number of protons and neutrons present in the nucleus. It can be written as:

Mass Number = Protons + Neutrons

Example

Carbon has an atomic number of 6, which means it contains 6 protons and 6 electrons in a neutral atom. If the number of neutrons is also 6, the mass number becomes 12.

Mass Number

Definition

The mass number is the total number of protons and neutrons present in the nucleus of an atom. It represents the overall mass of the nucleus because electrons have very small mass.

Formula

Mass Number = Number of Protons + Number of Neutrons

Relation with Atomic Number

The atomic number tells us the number of protons in an atom, while the mass number tells us the total number of protons and neutrons together. Using these two values, we can find the number of neutrons in an atom.

Neutrons = Mass Number − Atomic Number

Example

Sodium has an atomic number of 11 and a mass number of 23. Therefore, the number of neutrons in sodium is:

Neutrons = 23 − 11 = 12

Isotopes

Definition

Isotopes are atoms of the same element that have the same atomic number but different mass numbers. This happens because the number of neutrons in their nuclei is different, while the number of protons remains the same.

Key Characteristics

Since isotopes have the same atomic number, they show similar chemical properties. However, their physical properties such as mass and density may be different due to the change in neutron number.

Examples

Carbon has common isotopes such as Carbon-12 and Carbon-14. Both contain 6 protons, but Carbon-12 has 6 neutrons while Carbon-14 has 8 neutrons.

Uses of Isotopes

Some isotopes are useful in medicine, industry, and scientific research. For example, Carbon-14 is used to determine the age of ancient objects, and certain radioactive isotopes are used in medical diagnosis and treatment.

Electronic configuration

Definition

Electronic configuration is the arrangement of electrons in the different energy levels or shells around the nucleus of an atom. It helps us understand the chemical behavior of elements.

Shells and Their Capacity

Electrons are distributed in shells named K, L, M, and N. The maximum number of electrons that a shell can hold is given by the formula 2n², where n is the shell number.

  • K shell (n = 1): 2 electrons
  • L shell (n = 2): 8 electrons
  • M shell (n = 3): 18 electrons
  • N shell (n = 4): 32 electrons

Rules for Filling Electrons

Electrons are filled in shells step by step, starting from the innermost shell. The outermost shell cannot contain more than 8 electrons.

Examples

Sodium (Na) has atomic number 11. Its electronic configuration is:

2, 8, 1

Chlorine (Cl) has atomic number 17. Its electronic configuration is:

2, 8, 7

Valency

Definition

Valency is the combining capacity of an atom. It shows how many electrons an atom can lose, gain, or share while forming a chemical bond.

Relation with Electronic Configuration

Valency depends on the number of electrons present in the outermost shell (valence shell) of an atom. Atoms try to achieve a stable electronic configuration similar to noble gases, usually containing 8 electrons in the outer shell (octet rule).

Finding Valency

  • If outer electrons are 1, 2, or 3 → valency equals that number.
  • If outer electrons are 5, 6, or 7 → valency = 8 − outer electrons.
  • If outer shell is complete (8 electrons) → valency is 0.

Examples

Sodium (2,8,1) has valency 1 because it loses one electron.

Oxygen (2,6) has valency 2 because it gains two electrons.

Neon (2,8) has valency 0 because its outer shell is complete.

Importance of Valency

Valency helps in predicting how elements combine to form compounds and in writing correct chemical formulas.
